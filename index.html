Medical Negligence & Malpractice
Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005) 6 SCC 1Three-element test for criminal medical negligence. Bolam standard applies. Gross negligence threshold for BNS/IPC s.304A prosecution. Prior sanction of expert body recommended before criminal proceedings.
Kusum Sharma v. Batra Hospital (2010) 3 SCC 480Six-principle test for negligence. Expert medical evidence mandatory before prosecution. NCDRC jurisdiction affirmed.
Malay Kumar Ganguly v. Sukumar Mukherjee (2009) 9 SCC 221Civil and criminal liability can coexist. Hospital and doctor jointly liable. Punitive damages permissible for gross negligence.
Bolam v. Friern Hospital Management Committee [1957] 1 WLR 582Standard adopted by Indian courts. Not negligent if conduct accords with a responsible body of medical opinion.
Informed Consent
Samira Kohli v. Dr. Prabha Manchanda (2008) 2 SCC 1Informed consent doctrine in India. Consent must be specific to procedure performed. Extending procedure beyond consent = battery.
Pt. Parmanand Katara v. Union of India (1989) 4 SCC 286Right to emergency medical treatment. Duty to preserve life is paramount. Refusal violates Article 21.
Consumer Protection
IMA v. V.P. Shanta (1995) 6 SCC 651Medical profession within Consumer Protection Act. Patient as consumer. Free services excluded.
Spring Meadows Hospital v. Harjol Ahluwalia (1998) 4 SCC 39CPA extended to medical services. Hospital liable for negligence of staff.
Custodial Death & Article 21
D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997) 1 SCC 416Mandatory detention guidelines. Medical examination on arrest and release. Failure = contempt. Custodial death attracts strict Article 21 scrutiny.
Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa (1993) 2 SCC 746State vicariously liable for custodial death. Compensation under Art.32/226 is a public law remedy.

What is ROSS?

ROSS is a Medical Jurisprudence Intelligence System built exclusively for Indian law. It takes case facts and generates structured, court-presentable medico-legal analysis powered by Grok 4.

Every report is cryptographically sealed with a SHA-256 hash certificate, making it suitable as a verifiable electronic record under the Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023.

SHA-256 Document Integrity

Every ROSS report receives a SHA-256 cryptographic hash at the moment of generation. The hash covers the Report ID, timestamp, case input, and full report content. Any alteration to the document produces a different hash — proving tampering.

Legal basis: Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam 2023 §61-65 (electronic records), IT Act 2000 §85B (presumption of integrity for secure electronic records). Verification page available at /verify.html
