Medical Negligence & Malpractice
Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005) 6 SCC 1Three-element test for criminal medical negligence. Bolam standard applies. Gross negligence threshold required for BNS/IPC s.304A prosecution. Doctors protected from frivolous criminal proceedings — prior sanction of expert body recommended.
Kusum Sharma v. Batra Hospital (2010) 3 SCC 480Reiterated Jacob Mathew principles. Expert medical evidence mandatory before criminal prosecution. NCDRC jurisdiction over medical negligence affirmed. Six-principle test for negligence assessment.
Malay Kumar Ganguly v. Sukumar Mukherjee (2009) 9 SCC 221Civil and criminal liability can coexist in medical cases. Hospital and attending doctor jointly liable. Punitive damages may be awarded where negligence is gross.
Bolam v. Friern Hospital Management Committee [1957] 1 WLR 582UK standard adopted by Indian courts. Physician not negligent if conduct accords with a responsible body of medical opinion, even if another body holds a different view.
Informed Consent
Samira Kohli v. Dr. Prabha Manchanda (2008) 2 SCC 1Defined informed consent doctrine in India. Distinguished simple vs. informed consent. Consent must be specific to the procedure performed. Extending procedure beyond consent constitutes battery.
Pt. Parmanand Katara v. Union of India (1989) 4 SCC 286Every citizen has a right to emergency medical treatment regardless of ability to pay. Doctor's duty to preserve life is paramount. Refusal of emergency treatment violates Article 21.
Consumer Protection
Indian Medical Association v. V.P. Shanta (1995) 6 SCC 651Medical profession squarely within Consumer Protection Act 1986. Free services and government hospital services excluded. Patient as consumer — landmark ruling establishing CDRC jurisdiction.
Spring Meadows Hospital v. Harjol Ahluwalia (1998) 4 SCC 39Extended CPA to medical services. Minor patient treated through parents — both minor and parents are consumers. Hospital liable for negligence of staff.
Custodial Death & Article 21
D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997) 1 SCC 416Mandatory guidelines for arrest and detention. Mandatory medical examination on arrest and release. Right to inform family. Failure to follow guidelines = contempt of court. Custodial death attracts strict scrutiny under Article 21.
Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa (1993) 2 SCC 746State vicariously liable for custodial death. Compensation under Article 32/226 is a public law remedy distinct from private law damages. Fundamental right to life cannot be abridged even in custody.

What is ROSS?

ROSS is a Medical Jurisprudence Intelligence System built exclusively for Indian law. It applies forensic medicine, medical ethics, and Indian statutory frameworks to real-world case facts — generating structured, court-ready analysis for educational and research purposes.

It is designed for law students, researchers, and legal professionals who need to reason across medicine and law simultaneously — an intersection that is technically demanding and underserved by existing tools.
